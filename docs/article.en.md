# Software Engineering - Workplace English

## Preface

First, define your goal: do you want better workplace English, or do you want an English certificate.  
These are two different systems. This article focuses only on workplace English.

For software engineers, workplace English is usually easier to improve than people expect.  
Your communication scenarios are repetitive, and your topics are relatively stable.

My core point:  
**Do not use word-list memorization as your entry point.**  
Memorizing words without context is usually low-return effort.

---

## Practical Improvement Plan

I explain this with four parts: listening, speaking, reading, and writing.  
But the core levers are speaking and writing: listening is mainly unlocked by speaking, and grammar pain in reading is mainly solved by writing.

### 1) Listening

When you cannot understand spoken English, the root cause is often not "you listened too little," but "you cannot say it yourself."  
If you can pronounce a word correctly, you are much more likely to catch it when you hear it.

Conclusion:  
**Listening-only practice without speaking practice is usually ineffective; if you want better listening, train speaking first.**

---

### 2) Speaking

Do not start with random loud reading.  
If your pronunciation is wrong, repetition only reinforces the error.

In software engineering, many high-frequency words are often mispronounced, such as:  
`avatar` / `parameter` / `admin` / `claude` / `width`

Reference:  
[Chinese Programmer Wrong Pronunciation](https://github.com/shimohq/chinese-programmer-wrong-pronunciation)

Training has two levels:

#### a) Repeat

Use each sentence as a unit. Fully imitate the speaker:  
pronunciation, tone, pausing, rhythm, and emotion.

During repeat practice, clean up new words in context:  
check pronunciation, meaning, and sample usage.

#### b) Shadowing

After you can repeat well, move to shadowing.  
Speak simultaneously with the source audio and stay aligned with the speaker's style.

Use a paragraph as a unit.  
If you can complete a whole paragraph steadily, that round passes.

#### How to choose training materials

Start with materials that include **audio + transcript**. Work-related content is best; for beginners, daily-life or travel topics are also fine.

Good sources include:

- Listening practice apps (with transcripts)
- Shadowing courses on video platforms
- TV series (choose clear dialogues, moderate speed, and easy-to-find transcripts)

Selection rules:

- Do not pick content that is too hard: **no more than 2 new words per sentence**
- Short sentences with clear pauses, so you can repeat sentence by sentence

#### Listening + speaking notes

- Understanding usually starts from being able to read and say it correctly.
- Learn words in use, not in isolation.
- Choose easier materials: ideally no more than 2 unknown words per sentence.
- Do not translate first. Avoid the `Dog -> 狗 -> 🐶` chain, and train the direct mapping `Dog -> 🐶`.

Two advanced moves:

1. **Summary**: after a segment, restate it in your own words.  
2. Add massive listening only after you can repeat, shadow, and summarize.

---

### 3) Reading

Reading pain points are mostly about vocabulary and grammar.

My conclusion:  
**Reading does not need extra heavy training.**  
Software engineers already read English docs, errors, issues, and PRs every day.

How to improve:

- **Vocabulary**: learn words while reading real technical content.
- **Grammar**: I agree this is a real reading pain point, but the main fix is not "reading harder." The main fix is "continuous writing + feedback correction." Grammar grows from writing.

---

### 4) Writing

First remove the mental burden.  
At the beginning, you will have limited words, grammar mistakes, and Chinglish patterns.  
Do not wait. Start writing. Write for AI review first.

What to write:

- Self-introduction / role introduction
- Write a Harness adoption note (context, goal, implementation steps, risks, and rollback plan)
- A short design explanation or retrospective
- New hire OKR or task breakdown

Treat it as a workplace journal:  
one short piece per day, starting from around 120 words.

Who reviews it: AI.  
You can use this prompt:

> I am improving my workplace English through collaboration. I am a xxx.  
> Here is what I wrote. Please review and refine it with these requirements:  
> 1. Provide a revised sample based on my intent. Use short and simple sentences and avoid advanced words. Do not change my original meaning.  
> 2. Identify my mistakes, including spelling, phrase usage, tense, structure, and Chinglish expressions.  
> 3. Give collaboration guidance, including better wording and practical phrases I can reuse.

How to repeat:

- After AI review, do not rewrite the same text on the same day.
- Read the refined sample, corrections, and suggestions.
- On day 2, write new content and avoid yesterday's mistakes on purpose.
- Keep the loop: `write -> review -> refine`.

---

## One-Week Plan

- 30 minutes/day: choose material with audio + text and practice repeat (or shadowing if ready).
- 20 minutes/day: write about your real work from today (around 120 words).
- 10 minutes/day: read AI review and extract reusable expressions.

### What changes can appear in one week

- Lower speaking anxiety
- Better listening confidence
- Corrected pronunciation of common technical words
- Basic, readable English technical writing output
